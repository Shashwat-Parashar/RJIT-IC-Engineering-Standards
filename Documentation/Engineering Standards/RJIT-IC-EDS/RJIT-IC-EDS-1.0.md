# 1. Purpose

The purpose of the **RJIT Innovation Club Engineering Documentation Standard (RJIT-IC-EDS)** is to establish a unified engineering documentation framework for all technical projects undertaken by the RJIT Innovation Club.

This standard defines the requirements, conventions, and best practices for creating, reviewing, approving, publishing, and maintaining engineering documentation throughout the complete project lifecycle.

By adopting a common documentation standard, the club aims to:

- Improve consistency across engineering projects.
- Promote professional engineering practices.
- Ensure technical information remains accurate and traceable.
- Preserve engineering knowledge for future club members.
- Improve collaboration between multidisciplinary teams.
- Provide reusable documentation templates and workflows.
- Support project continuity beyond individual contributors.

RJIT-IC-EDS serves as the governing document for all engineering documentation produced within the repository and should be referenced whenever new documentation is created or existing documentation is revised.


# 2. Scope

This standard applies to all engineering documentation developed under the RJIT Innovation Club, regardless of project discipline or team size.

RJIT-IC-EDS governs the preparation, organization, revision, approval, publication, and archival of engineering documents generated throughout the project lifecycle.

The standard applies to, but is not limited to:

- Project proposals
- Design specifications
- Engineering drawings
- CAD models and related documentation
- Software documentation
- Source code documentation
- Testing and validation reports
- Engineering calculations
- Meeting minutes
- Bills of Materials (BOM)
- User manuals
- Maintenance documentation
- Final project reports
- Engineering change documentation

This standard applies to projects involving:

- Mechanical Engineering
- Electronics Engineering
- Electrical Engineering
- Embedded Systems
- Software Engineering
- Robotics
- Artificial Intelligence
- Internet of Things (IoT)
- Biomedical Engineering
- Aerospace and Aviation
- Interdisciplinary Engineering Projects

The standard shall be followed by all club members contributing to engineering documentation and should be adopted for all officially recognized Innovation Club projects.

Documents produced outside the scope of engineering activities, such as administrative notices, financial records, and general club communications, are not governed by this standard unless explicitly stated.


# 3. Definitions

For the purposes of this standard, the following definitions apply.

---

## 3.1 Engineering Documentation

Documents that communicate engineering information throughout the lifecycle of a project, including planning, design, analysis, manufacturing, testing, deployment, maintenance, and archival.

---

## 3.2 Document Control

The process of creating, reviewing, approving, revising, distributing, and archiving engineering documents to ensure accuracy, consistency, and traceability.

---

## 3.3 Revision

A controlled modification made to an existing document.

Each revision shall be uniquely identified and recorded in the document's revision history.

---

## 3.4 Version

A numbered release of a document or standard representing a defined state of development.

**Examples:**

- Version 1.0.0
- Version 1.1.0
- Version 2.0.0

---

## 3.5 Template

A predefined document structure intended to standardize the preparation of engineering documentation.

---

## 3.6 Repository

The official version-controlled storage location containing engineering documentation, templates, standards, and supporting resources.

---

## 3.7 Maintainer

An individual authorized to review, approve, manage, and publish documentation within the repository.

---

## 3.8 Contributor

Any individual who creates, reviews, or improves documentation within the repository.

---

## 3.9 Technology Demonstrator (TD)

A prototype or experimental system developed to demonstrate the technical feasibility of a concept, technology, or engineering approach without representing the final production design.

---

## 3.10 Prototype

An initial implementation of a product, component, or system developed to evaluate design concepts, functionality, manufacturability, and performance prior to final production.

---

## 3.11 Subsystem

A distinct functional portion of a larger engineering system designed to perform a specific set of tasks while interacting with other subsystems.

---

## 3.12 Requirement

A documented capability, characteristic, constraint, or performance criterion that a system or component must satisfy.

---

## 3.13 Specification

A formal document describing the technical requirements, characteristics, performance criteria, and constraints of a product, system, or component.

---

## 3.14 Verification Report

A document recording the activities and results used to confirm that engineering requirements have been satisfied.

---

## 3.15 Validation Report

A document demonstrating that the completed system fulfills its intended operational purpose and user needs.

---

## 3.16 Configuration

The defined arrangement of hardware, software, documentation, and associated components representing a controlled state of an engineering project.

---

## 3.17 Engineering Drawing

A technical document that graphically communicates the geometry, dimensions, tolerances, materials, and manufacturing information required to produce a component or assembly.

---

## 3.18 Lifecycle

The complete sequence of stages through which an engineering project or product progresses, from concept and planning through design, development, testing, deployment, maintenance, and retirement.

# 4. Project Lifecycle

## 4.1 Purpose

The Engineering Project Lifecycle establishes a standardized framework for planning, developing, documenting, reviewing, and maintaining projects undertaken by the RJIT Innovation Club.

The lifecycle defines the sequence of activities and the corresponding documentation required at each stage of a project. Its purpose is to promote consistency, improve collaboration, ensure technical traceability, and preserve knowledge throughout the complete lifecycle of a project.

All officially recognized Innovation Club projects should follow this lifecycle unless an alternative process has been approved by the project mentor or repository maintainers.

---

## 4.2 Lifecycle Overview

Every project shall progress through the following lifecycle phases:

1. Concept
2. Planning
3. Design
4. Development
5. Testing
6. Deployment
7. Maintenance
8. Archive

Each phase produces documentation that serves as the foundation for subsequent phases.

---

## 4.3 Lifecycle Phases

### 4.3.1 Concept

The Concept phase identifies a problem or opportunity and evaluates the feasibility of a proposed engineering solution.

Typical activities include:

- Problem identification
- Background research
- Literature review
- Feasibility analysis
- Initial brainstorming
- Definition of project objectives

---

### 4.3.2 Planning

The Planning phase defines how the project will be executed and managed.

Typical activities include:

- Scope definition
- Task planning
- Resource allocation
- Budget estimation
- Timeline preparation
- Team assignment

---

### 4.3.3 Design

The Design phase develops the technical solution.

Typical activities include:

- System architecture
- Mechanical design
- Electrical design
- Electronics design
- Software architecture
- CAD modelling
- Design reviews

---

### 4.3.4 Development

The Development phase transforms the approved design into a functional prototype or engineering system.

Typical activities include:

- Manufacturing
- Fabrication
- Assembly
- Programming
- PCB development
- Integration

---

### 4.3.5 Testing

The Testing phase verifies that the system satisfies engineering requirements and validates that it fulfills its intended purpose.

Typical activities include:

- Functional testing
- Performance testing
- Safety testing
- Integration testing
- User evaluation

---

### 4.3.6 Deployment

The Deployment phase prepares the project for demonstration, competition, operational use, or handover.

Typical activities include:

- Final assembly
- User training
- Documentation completion
- Presentation preparation

---

### 4.3.7 Maintenance

The Maintenance phase manages improvements, corrections, and revisions following deployment.

Typical activities include:

- Bug fixes
- Engineering changes
- Documentation updates
- Component replacement
- Performance improvements

---

### 4.3.8 Archive

The Archive phase preserves project knowledge for future reference and reuse.

Typical activities include:

- Final documentation review
- Repository archival
- Knowledge transfer
- Lessons learned

---

## 4.4 Lifecycle Principles

All engineering projects should:

- Progress through the lifecycle in a logical sequence.
- Maintain documentation throughout each phase.
- Review and approve documentation before advancing to the next phase.
- Record significant engineering decisions and revisions.
- Preserve completed documentation for future reference and knowledge transfer.

The lifecycle defined in this standard serves as the recommended engineering workflow for all RJIT Innovation Club projects.

# 7. Revision Control

## 7.1 Purpose

Revision Control establishes a standardized method for managing changes to engineering documents throughout their lifecycle.

Its purpose is to ensure that only the latest approved version of a document is used while preserving the history of previous revisions.

---

## 7.2 Revision Principles

- Every controlled document shall maintain a revision history.
- Changes shall be documented clearly and accurately.
- Previous revisions shall be retained for reference.
- Only the latest approved revision shall be considered the official version.

---

## 7.3 Revision Numbering

Document revisions shall follow a simple sequential numbering system.

**Examples:**

```
Rev 0
Rev 1
Rev 2
Rev 3
```

Revision numbers shall increase whenever significant changes are made to a controlled document.

---

## 7.4 Revision History

Each controlled document shall include a Revision History table near the beginning of the document.

Example:

| Revision | Date | Description | Author |
|----------|------|-------------|--------|
| Rev 0 | 09 Jul 2026 | Initial Release | RJIT Innovation Club |
| Rev 1 | 15 Jul 2026 | Updated Design Requirements | John Doe |

---

## 7.5 Minor Corrections

Minor corrections, such as spelling, formatting, or grammatical improvements, may be included in the next scheduled revision unless an immediate update is required.

---

## 7.6 Major Revisions

Major revisions include changes that affect:

- Project requirements
- Technical specifications
- Engineering calculations
- Design decisions
- Safety requirements
- Testing procedures

Major revisions shall be recorded in the Revision History.

---

## 7.7 General Requirements

- Every official document shall contain revision information.
- Revision history shall remain with the document throughout its lifecycle.
- Superseded revisions shall be retained for historical reference.

# 9. Figures, Tables, and Equations

## 9.1 Purpose

This chapter establishes the requirements for numbering, captioning, and referencing figures, tables, and equations within engineering documents.

Consistent presentation improves readability, clarity, and professionalism.

---

## 9.2 Figures

Figures include photographs, diagrams, graphs, charts, flowcharts, schematics, CAD renderings, and illustrations.

Requirements:

- Every figure shall have a unique number.
- Every figure shall include a descriptive caption.
- Figures shall be referenced within the document text before they appear.
- Figure captions shall be placed below the figure.

Example:

```
Figure 1.0. System Architecture of the Robotic Arm
```

---

## 9.3 Tables

Tables present structured engineering information such as specifications, calculations, component lists, and test results.

Requirements:

- Every table shall have a unique number.
- Every table shall include a descriptive title.
- Tables shall be referenced within the document text before they appear.
- Table titles shall be placed above the table.

Example:

```
Table 1.2. Motor Specifications
```

---

## 9.4 Equations

Equations shall be presented clearly and numbered sequentially when referenced within the document.

Requirements:

- Equations shall be centered where practical.
- Equation numbers shall appear on the right side of the page.
- Variables and symbols shall be defined following the equation where necessary.

Example:

```
                F = ma                                  (1.4)
```

Where:

- F = Force (N)
- m = Mass (kg)
- a = Acceleration (m/s²)

---

## 9.5 Numbering

Figures, tables, and equations shall each maintain separate sequential numbering.

Examples:

```
Figure 1.4
Figure 2.1

Table 1.1
Table 2.0

Equation (1.0)
Equation (2.1)
```

---

## 9.6 References

Figures, tables, and equations shall be referenced by their assigned numbers.

Examples:

- As shown in Figure 3...
- Refer to Table 2...
- Using Equation (4)...

Avoid using phrases such as:

- The figure below
- The table above
- The equation on the next page

---

## 9.7 General Requirements

- Figures and tables shall be clear and legible.
- Graphics shall maintain sufficient resolution for printing and digital viewing.
- Decorative graphics that do not contribute to the technical content should be avoided.
- Every figure, table, and equation shall support the engineering content of the document.


# 10. References and Citations

## 10.1 Purpose

This chapter establishes the requirements for citing and referencing external sources used in engineering documents prepared under the RJIT Innovation Club Engineering Documentation Standard (RJIT-IC-EDS).

Proper citation acknowledges the work of others, supports technical decisions, and enables readers to locate the original sources.

---

## 10.2 General Requirements

- All information obtained from external sources shall be appropriately cited.
- Every in-text citation shall have a corresponding entry in the References section.
- Every reference listed shall be cited within the document.
- A single citation style shall be used consistently throughout a document.

---

## 10.3 Acceptable Sources

Engineering documents may reference:

- Engineering standards
- Research papers
- Technical reports
- Books
- Manufacturer datasheets
- Official documentation
- Government publications
- Reputable academic and technical websites

Sources of uncertain reliability should be avoided.

---

## 10.4 Citation Style

Unless otherwise specified by a project, competition, sponsor, or academic institution, the IEEE citation style is recommended for engineering documents.

Example:

```
...as described in [1].
```

---

## 10.5 References

The References section shall appear near the end of the document.

References should be complete, accurate, and sufficient to identify the original source.

---

## 10.6 General Requirements

- References shall be relevant to the document.
- Citations shall accurately represent the original source.
- Plagiarism is strictly prohibited.
- All referenced material shall comply with applicable copyright and licensing requirements.

Dimensions shall:

- PDF
